<p><i><b>Flower</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/Thatgamecompany" title="Thatgamecompany">Thatgamecompany</a> and published by <a href="/wiki/Sony_Computer_Entertainment" class="mw-redirect" title="Sony Computer Entertainment">Sony Computer Entertainment</a> for the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a>. It was designed by <a href="/wiki/Jenova_Chen" title="Jenova Chen">Jenova Chen</a> and Nicholas Clark and was released in February 2009 on the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Network" title="PlayStation Network">PlayStation Network</a>. <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_4" title="PlayStation 4">PlayStation 4</a> and <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Vita" title="PlayStation Vita">PlayStation Vita</a> versions of the game were <a href="/wiki/Porting" title="Porting">ported</a> by <a href="/wiki/Bluepoint_Games" title="Bluepoint Games">Bluepoint Games</a> and released in November 2013. An <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a> version was released in September 2017, and a <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows</a> version was released in February 2019, both published by <a href="/wiki/Annapurna_Interactive" title="Annapurna Interactive">Annapurna Interactive</a>. The game was intended as a "<a href="/wiki/Spiritual_successor" title="Spiritual successor">spiritual successor</a>" to <i><a href="/wiki/Flow_(video_game)" title="Flow (video game)">Flow</a></i>, a previous title by Chen and Thatgamecompany. In <i>Flower</i>, the player controls the wind, blowing a flower <a href="/wiki/Petal" title="Petal">petal</a> through the air using the movement of the game controller. Flying close to flowers results in the player's petal being followed by other flower petals. Approaching flowers may also have side-effects on the game world, such as bringing vibrant color to previously dead fields or activating stationary <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbines</a>. The game features no text or dialogue, forming a narrative arc primarily through visual representation and emotional cues.
</p><p><i>Flower</i> was primarily intended to arouse positive emotions in the player, rather than to be a challenging and "fun" game. This focus was sparked by Chen, who felt that the primary purpose of entertainment products like video games was the feelings that they evoked in the audience and that the emotional range of most games was very limited. The team viewed their efforts as creating a work of art, removing gameplay elements and mechanics that were not provoking the desired response in the players. The music, composed by Vincent Diamante, dynamically responds to the player's actions and corresponds with the emotional cues in the game. <i>Flower</i> was a critical success, to the surprise of the developers. Reviewers praised the game's music, visuals, and gameplay, calling it a unique and compelling emotional experience. It was named the "best independent game of 2009" at the <a href="/wiki/Spike_Video_Game_Awards" title="Spike Video Game Awards">Spike Video Game Awards</a>, and won the "<a href="/wiki/D.I.C.E._Award_for_Outstanding_Achievement_for_an_Independent_Game" title="D.I.C.E. Award for Outstanding Achievement for an Independent Game">Casual Game of the Year</a>" award by the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Interactive_Arts_and_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Academy of Interactive Arts and Sciences">Academy of Interactive Arts and Sciences</a>.
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Gameplay">Gameplay</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flower_(video_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gameplay"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Flower_screen01.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img alt="A group of pink flower petals is displayed above a green grassy field with the viewer seemingly amongst them. The sky is pink-toned, and a light yellow sun is shown above the horizon on the right side." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f5/Flower_screen01.jpg/250px-Flower_screen01.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="140" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f5/Flower_screen01.jpg/375px-Flower_screen01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/f/f5/Flower_screen01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="421" data-file-height="236" /></a><figcaption>A screenshot of <i>Flower</i>, showing a trail of flower petals as viewed by the player as they are blown through the air.</figcaption></figure>
<p><i>Flower</i> is divided up into six main levels and one <a href="/wiki/Closing_credits" title="Closing credits">credits</a> level. Each level is represented by a flower in a pot on a city apartment windowsill, and upon selecting one the player is taken to the "dream" of that flower.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1upRev&#95;3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1upRev-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Once inside a level, the player controls the wind as it blows a single flower petal through the air. Changes in the <a href="/wiki/Flight_dynamics" title="Flight dynamics">pitch and roll</a> of the floating petal are accomplished by tilting the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a> controller. Pressing any button blows the wind harder, which in turn moves the petal faster.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GPreview&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GPreview-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The camera generally follows just behind the petal, though it sometimes moves to show a new objective or consequence of the player's actions.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GTreview&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GTreview-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Groups and lines of flowers are present in each level; approaching these with the petal causes them to bloom and a new petal to trail the first. When the player approaches certain flowers or groups of flowers, changes are made to the game world. These include opening new areas, transforming dead grassy areas to bright green fields, or activating wind turbines. These changes generally result in new flowers sprouting for the player to interact with. Flying through each flower results in a musical chime that harmonizes with the music. The music itself dynamically adjusts as changes are made to the world.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GPreview&#95;4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GPreview-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The more flower petals the player has trailing the lead petal, the faster the petals move.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1upRev&#95;3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1upRev-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It is impossible for the player to lose a level or any progress. The game features no enemies, <a href="/wiki/Health_(gaming)" class="mw-redirect" title="Health (gaming)">hit points</a>, or time limits. A single play-through of the game takes approximately one hour.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GTreview&#95;5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GTreview-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Although no speech or text is used anywhere in the game aside from credits and interaction hints in the main menu, the six flower dreams follow a narrative arc.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GPreview&#95;4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GPreview-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The player's starting location in each stage appears to be near the ending location of the previous one, and through the course of the game the player approaches a distant city. The first levels focus on restoring life and color to the landscape. After activating a series of windmills, the player flies through a nighttime field, illuminating darkened strings of lights until they reach the city. The city is full of menacing metal structures similar to <a href="/wiki/Lattice_tower" title="Lattice tower">lattice towers</a>, small arcs of electricity, and washed-out buildings; the player enlivens the city in the final two levels and transforms it into a bright and cheerful place.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-WIREDblog&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WIREDblog-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-INDIEreview&#95;7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-INDIEreview-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>As the player progresses through the different levels of the game, the city viewed through the apartment window in the level selection screen gradually becomes more vibrant and colorful.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-WIREDblog&#95;6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WIREDblog-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> If the player triggers three secret flowers in each level, the cityscape is replaced with a bright field with mountains in the background. The music changes in scope as the game progresses, growing in scale and complexity and adding to the narrative arc.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GAMAmusic&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GAMAmusic-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The credits level is played in a similar manner to the main levels, but as the player flies through each flower the name of a person involved in the game appears above it. <i>Flower</i> includes <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Network#Trophies" title="PlayStation Network">PlayStation Network trophies</a> in keeping with the game's feel. While some are objective-based, many are centered on relaxing and watching the scenery.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GTreview&#95;5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GTreview-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Development">Development</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flower_(video_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Jenova_Chen_-_2007.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Jenova_Chen_-_2007.jpg/250px-Jenova_Chen_-_2007.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="200" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Jenova_Chen_-_2007.jpg/330px-Jenova_Chen_-_2007.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="3456" /></a><figcaption>Designer Jenova Chen in 2007</figcaption></figure>
<p><i>Flower</i> was developed as a spiritual successor to <i><a href="/wiki/Flow_(video_game)" title="Flow (video game)">Flow</a></i>, a 2006 <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Flash" title="Adobe Flash">Flash</a> game created by <a href="/wiki/Jenova_Chen" title="Jenova Chen">Jenova Chen</a> and Nicholas Clark while the two were students at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">University of Southern California</a>. <i>Flow</i> was later developed into a PlayStation 3 game by <a href="/wiki/Thatgamecompany" title="Thatgamecompany">Thatgamecompany</a> in 2007 and a <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Portable" title="PlayStation Portable">PlayStation Portable</a> game by <a href="/wiki/SuperVillain_Studios,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="SuperVillain Studios, Inc.">SuperVillain Studios</a> in 2008. <i>Flower</i> was Thatgamecompany's "first game outside the safety net of academia".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GAMAdevint&#95;9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GAMAdevint-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It was first announced at the <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Game_Show" title="Tokyo Game Show">Tokyo Game Show</a> on September 24, 2007,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GAMAdevint&#95;9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GAMAdevint-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and was released on the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Network" title="PlayStation Network">PlayStation Network</a> on February 12, 2009.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Release&#95;10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Release-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Price&#95;11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Price-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <i>Flower</i> was intended primarily to provoke positive emotions in the player, and to act as "an emotional shelter".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GAMAdevint2&#95;12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GAMAdevint2-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Six to nine people were involved at varying stages of development.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GAMAdevint2&#95;12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GAMAdevint2-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Chen, who co-founded Thatgamecompany with game producer <a href="/wiki/Kellee_Santiago" title="Kellee Santiago">Kellee Santiago</a>, was the creative director in charge of the game, while Clark was the lead designer.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-TGCabout&#95;13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TGCabout-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Chen described the game as "an interactive poem exploring the tension between urban and nature".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Release&#95;10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Release-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He decided on the "nature" theme early in the development process, saying that he "had this concept that every PlayStation is like a portal in your living room, it leads you to somewhere else. I thought; wouldn't it be nice if it was a portal that would allow you to be embraced by nature".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-EXCLAIMint&#95;14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EXCLAIMint-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Before beginning work, the development team commissioned two pieces of music that they felt would inspire the right emotional tone for the game to guide their efforts.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GAMAdevint&#95;9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GAMAdevint-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> They created a number of <a href="/wiki/Prototype" title="Prototype">prototypes</a>, including concepts focused on growing flowers and based around human consciousness. The team decided that a prototype centered on petals floating in the wind best captured the emotions they wanted to evoke. They made keeping the player in a peaceful emotional state their design focus, and removed elements that frustrated players such as petal collection requirements to unlock levels and game mechanics that were too traditional and made the players too excited.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GAMAdevint2&#95;12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GAMAdevint2-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The team tried to not place any barriers in the levels, allowing the player to go anywhere in an open world but realized that without a few guidelines, such as the camera focusing on new flowers or segmenting the levels, players became confused and frustrated. Chen described the process as "almost like we wanted to throw away the traditional game design, but we end up picking up all the pieces we threw away and putting them back because we know those are actually needed to deliver a good guided experience".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GAMACHENint2&#95;15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GAMACHENint2-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The overall development time was two years, but the team spent three quarters of that time in the prototyping stage. After deciding on the game elements, <i>Flower</i> was produced in only six months.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GAMASANTint&#95;16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GAMASANTint-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The game's focus on emotions was sparked by Chen, who felt that the primary purpose of entertainment products like video games was the feelings that they evoked in the audience and that the emotional range of most games was very limited. Chen tried to make the game focus more on emotions than on a message; he specifically changed the design of Flower when early testers felt there was a message of promoting green energy in the game.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GIint&#95;17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GIint-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> To make <i>Flower</i> have the "emotional spectrum" that he wanted, Chen looked at the development process as creating a work of art, rather than a "fun" game, which would not provoke the desired emotions.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GAMACHENint&#95;18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GAMACHENint-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He summarized this view by saying that the only gameplay mechanic is hitting a flower to trigger a new event. The team specifically cut out deeper gameplay elements because these would have added "challenge" to the game, which, while fun, would not have been relaxing.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GAMACHENint2&#95;15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GAMACHENint2-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Santa_Monica_Studio" title="Santa Monica Studio">Santa Monica Studio</a> contracted with <a href="/wiki/Bluepoint_Games" title="Bluepoint Games">Bluepoint Games</a> to create ports of the game for the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_4" title="PlayStation 4">PlayStation 4</a> and <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Vita" title="PlayStation Vita">PlayStation Vita</a>, which were published in November 2013 to correspond with the release of the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_4" title="PlayStation 4">PlayStation 4</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-PS4version&#95;19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PS4version-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-SonyBlog&#95;20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SonyBlog-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Annapurna_Interactive" title="Annapurna Interactive">Annapurna Interactive</a> brought <i>Flower</i> to the <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a> platform in September 2017, and to <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a> in February 2019.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-iosRelease&#95;21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iosRelease-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<p>The music for <i>Flower</i> was created by Vincent Diamante, a video game music composer and professor at the University of Southern California's <a href="/wiki/USC_Interactive_Media_Division" class="mw-redirect" title="USC Interactive Media Division">Interactive Media Division</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-VDsite&#95;23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VDsite-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He had previously scored the music for Chen's first game <i><a href="/wiki/Cloud_(video_game)" title="Cloud (video game)">Cloud</a></i> (2005), and <i>Dyadin</i> (2005), when he and Chen were both at the University of Southern California. He worked directly with the development team to integrate the music into the game by adjusting the placements of flowers and the tones that each type played when they were reached. He did this by harmonizing the gameplay with the music, and adjusting the music dynamically to correspond to changes in the game world.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GAMAmusic&#95;8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GAMAmusic-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Diamante used his music to influence the development team in adapting ideas he had for the game.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GAMAmusictalk&#95;24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GAMAmusictalk-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The music and instruments in each level were chosen to correspond to the game world and the level's placement in the overall emotional arc. The music is composed of multiple layers of acoustic instrument tracks that rise and fall in correlation with the player's actions.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GAMAmusic&#95;8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GAMAmusic-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The instruments used include pianos, string instruments such as classical guitars, and woodwinds such as bass flutes and <a href="/wiki/Bassoons" class="mw-redirect" title="Bassoons">bassoons</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GAMAmusic&#95;8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GAMAmusic-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-1UPmusic&#95;25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1UPmusic-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The number of instruments playing increases in correlation with the amount of petals that the player gathers.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1UPmusic&#95;25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1UPmusic-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The music is meant to suggest natural sounds like wind. The instrument tracks were intended to be able to stand on their own, even when used in a large orchestrated group as in levels three and six.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GAMAmusic&#95;8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GAMAmusic-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Several times in the development process, Diamante became so attached to a piece of music that needed alteration that he composed a new piece to replace it.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GAMAmusictalk&#95;24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GAMAmusictalk-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Diamante was long in talks with Sony to produce an album of music from the game.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GAMAmusic&#95;8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GAMAmusic-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-VDsite&#95;23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VDsite-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On April 8, 2010, a soundtrack album was released by Sony for purchase on the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Network" title="PlayStation Network">PlayStation Network</a>. The album, titled <i>Flower: Original Soundtrack from the Video Game</i>, contains 8 tracks with a total duration of 1:04:37.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-FlowerOST&#95;26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FlowerOST-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<p><i>Flower</i> was well received by critics. Alice Liang of <i><a href="/wiki/1UP.com" class="mw-redirect" title="1UP.com">1UP.com</a></i> applauded the game, saying that "the freedom of movement makes the game feel as relaxing as a gently wafting breeze". She felt that "fun" did not fully describe the game experience, saying that it had a "well-constructed movie's emotional arc" and that the game's music, visuals, and gameplay all drew the player into a compelling emotional experience. She also felt that it was very replayable, which offset its brevity.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1upRev&#95;3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1upRev-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Ryan Clements of <i><a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a></i> agreed with Liang's opinion, saying that it provided "more enjoyment, emotion and enlightenment than any game" he had played in years. Though he noted that the game would not appeal to everyone, he described it as "something very unique and very powerful", referring to it as a "must-play".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-IGNRev&#95;30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGNRev-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Michael Kontoudis of <i>PALGN</i> called it an "utterly unique, brave and moving game brimming with personality and intent", though he noted that as it was closer to a work of art than a game, many players would not be interested in it.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-PGNreview&#95;31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PGNreview-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Gerard Campbell of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Press" title="The Press">The Press</a></i> similarly described it as something more than a game, calling it a "perfect foil" to "ultra-violent shoot-'em-up" games and summarizing it as "one of the most refreshing and relaxing games around".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-TIMESreview&#95;33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TIMESreview-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Eurogamer" title="Eurogamer">Eurogamer</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;s</span> Tom Bramwell had similar praise, describing it as "pleasantly innocent and uplifting", though he awarded it a lower score than other reviewers as he felt the US$9.99 price was too high for the game's length.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-EuroGRev&#95;29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EuroGRev-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This criticism was not universal, as reviewers such as Jason Hill of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Age" title="The Age">The Age</a></i> called the Australian price of <span style="white-space: nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Australian_dollar" title="Australian dollar">A$</a>13</span> "reasonable" and described the length as not "overstay[ing] its welcome".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-AGEreview&#95;34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AGEreview-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Critics such as <i><a href="/wiki/GamePro" title="GamePro">GamePro</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;s</span> Terry Terrones and <i><a href="/wiki/GameTrailers" title="GameTrailers">GameTrailers</a></i> echoed the same praises as other reviewers for the game; <i>GameTrailers</i> said that it was "less a game and more an experience. You don't necessarily 'play' <i>Flower</i>; you interact with it", while Terrones noted the music as the best part of the game's presentation.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GPreview&#95;4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GPreview-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-GTreview&#95;5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GTreview-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Tom Hoggins of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i> felt that <i>Flower</i> would "reignite the 'video games as art' debate" and was of the opinion that it was a "wonderful work of art" in addition to a game.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-TELEGRAPHreview&#95;35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TELEGRAPHreview-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Kevin Vanord of <i><a href="/wiki/GameSpot" title="GameSpot">GameSpot</a></i>, in reviewing the 2013 PlayStation 4 version, said that the game "invites introspection and inner calm", and that it was a type of game experience that was still rare four years after it first came out.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GSreview&#95;32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GSreview-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The positive reception surprised the developers, as they had expected a mixed reaction.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GAMACHENint2&#95;15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GAMACHENint2-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p><i>Flower</i> received the "Best Independent Game Fueled by Dew" award at the 2009 <a href="/wiki/Spike_Video_Game_Awards" title="Spike Video Game Awards">Spike Video Game Awards</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Spike&#95;36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spike-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It was similarly named the "Best Indie Game" of 2009 by <i><a href="/wiki/Playboy" title="Playboy">Playboy</a></i>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-TGCflower&#95;37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TGCflower-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <i>Flower</i> was awarded "<a href="/wiki/D.I.C.E._Award_for_Outstanding_Achievement_for_an_Independent_Game" title="D.I.C.E. Award for Outstanding Achievement for an Independent Game">Casual Game of the Year</a>" at the <a href="/wiki/13th_Annual_Interactive_Achievement_Awards" title="13th Annual Interactive Achievement Awards">13th Annual Interactive Achievement Awards</a>; it also received nominations for "Outstanding Innovation in Gaming", and outstanding achievement in "<a href="/wiki/D.I.C.E._Award_for_Outstanding_Achievement_in_Audio_Design" title="D.I.C.E. Award for Outstanding Achievement in Audio Design">Sound Design</a>", "<a href="/wiki/D.I.C.E._Award_for_Outstanding_Achievement_in_Original_Music_Composition" title="D.I.C.E. Award for Outstanding Achievement in Original Music Composition">Original Music Composition</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/D.I.C.E._Award_for_Outstanding_Achievement_in_Game_Direction" title="D.I.C.E. Award for Outstanding Achievement in Game Direction">Game Direction</a>".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-AIAS&#95;38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIAS-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It was nominated for the "Use of Audio" <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_Video_Games_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="British Academy Video Games Awards">video game awards</a> by the <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_of_Film_and_Television_Arts" title="British Academy of Film and Television Arts">British Academy of Film and Television Arts</a> and won the "Artistic Achievement" award.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-BAFTA&#95;39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BAFTA-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Its soundtrack was awarded the 2009 "Best Original Soundtrack" prize from <a href="/wiki/G4_(U.S._TV_channel)" class="mw-redirect" title="G4 (U.S. TV channel)">G4</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-G4award&#95;40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G4award-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The music was also nominated for the Game Audio Network Guild's "Music of the Year" award and won the "Best Interactive Score" award.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-GANG&#95;41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GANG-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-GANG2&#95;42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GANG2-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Its gameplay debut at the 2008 <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Entertainment_Expo" class="mw-redirect" title="Electronic Entertainment Expo">Electronic Entertainment Expo</a> resulted in multiple awards, including "Best E3 Download Game" from <i><a href="/wiki/1UP.com" class="mw-redirect" title="1UP.com">1UP.com</a></i>, "Best Original Game" from <i><a href="/wiki/UGO" class="mw-redirect" title="UGO">UGO</a></i>, and "Special Achievement for Innovation" from <i><a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a></i>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1UPE3award&#95;43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1UPE3award-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-UGOE3award&#95;44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UGOE3award-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-IGNE3award&#95;45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGNE3award-45"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2012, <i>Flower</i> was listed on <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;s</span> All-Time 100 greatest video games list.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Time100&#95;46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Time100-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>45<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, <i>Flower</i> was chosen through a public vote out of an initial selection of 240 to be one of 80 games showcased in a 2012 exhibit at the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_American_Art_Museum" title="Smithsonian American Art Museum">Smithsonian American Art Museum</a> titled "<a href="/wiki/The_Art_of_Video_Games" title="The Art of Video Games">The Art of Video Games</a>".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-SMITHexhibit&#95;47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SMITHexhibit-47"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2013, the museum acquired the game for its permanent collection.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-SMITHperm&#95;48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SMITHperm-48"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>47<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The game was showcased in the 2015 Smithsonian exhibition, <i>Watch This! Revelations in Media Art</i>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-SMITHwatch&#95;49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SMITHwatch-49"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>48<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
